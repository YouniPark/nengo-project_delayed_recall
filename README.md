Abstract
Traumatic brain injury (TBI) frequently results in memory impairments, particularly in delayed recall tasks that rely on effective encoding of new information. To investigate the mechanistic contribution of encoding-related factors, we developed a simplified recurrent spiking neural network in Nengo inspired by the California Verbal Learning Test Second Edition Short Form (CVLT-II SF). The model encodes 16 sequentially presented words as high-dimensional semantic pointers, binding content and temporal order through circular convolution and maintaining activity over a 30-second delay using recurrent connections with low-pass synaptic filtering. Memory performance was quantified as the proportion of words correctly recalled in order.
Three architectural parameters were systematically varied: neuron number, representational dimensionality, and input scaling. Across thirty baseline trials, recall accuracy remained stable over delays from 0 to 30 seconds (p = 0.3758, η²ₚ = 0.0096), indicating that temporal decay was not a primary determinant of performance in the current architecture. Neuron number had a minimal and non-significant effect (p = 0.332, η²ₚ = 0.117), with accuracy fluctuating irregularly across tested values. In contrast, representational dimensionality exerted a strong and highly significant influence (p = 1.18 × 10⁻¹⁵, η²ₚ = 0.889), demonstrating an approximately linear improvement in accuracy as dimensionality increased. Input scaling also significantly affected performance (p = 3.27 × 10⁻¹¹, η²ₚ = 0.407), with moderate reductions relative to baseline improving recall. A candidate configuration (n_neurons = 500, dim = 768, scale = 3.66) yielded consistently higher accuracy than baseline across delays but remained far below human-level performance.
These findings suggest that encoding capacity and representational richness, rather than retention duration or network size alone, can be primary determinants of recall performance in this model.
Introduction
A traumatic brain injury (TBI) is defined as damage to the brain caused by an external force [1]. This can result from a bump, blow, or jolt to the head or body, although not every impact leads to a TBI [1]. Penetrating injuries, such as when an object enters the brain, can also cause TBI [1]. Certain types of TBI may produce temporary impairments in brain function, affecting cognition, comprehension, movement, communication, and behavior [1]. More severe TBIs can result in lasting disabilities or even death [1]. Moderate and severe TBI can lead to lifelong physical, cognitive, emotional, and behavioral changes that significantly impact daily functioning [2]. Cognitive deficits are among the most common and debilitating consequences, particularly impairments in the ability to learn and remember new information [3].
TBI is a leading cause of death and disability worldwide [4]. In the United States, over 69,000 TBI-related deaths occurred in 2021, averaging approximately 190 deaths per day [5]. Nearly 10 million Americans reported sustaining a TBI between 2022 and 2023, affecting 2.2 % of children and 3.3 % of adults [6]. In Canada, between 2002 and 2016, there were 235,471 injury-related deaths, of which 53,200 (22.6%) involved a TBI diagnosis [7]. Each year, over 20,000 people in Canada are hospitalized for TBI, ranging from mild to severe injuries, including concussions [7].
Memory impairments following TBI are most pronounced in delayed recall and tasks requiring strategic encoding and retrieval, frequently affecting learning rate and prospective memory [8]-[10]. These deficits resemble those observed in patients with frontal lobe damage and are not typically associated with storage problems seen in conditions like Alzheimer’s Disease or global amnesia [8], [11]-[13]. Encoding deficits may result in misattribution of information or the conflation of details from different sources [9]. Such impairments are most evident in explicit memory, which involves conscious retrieval of past experiences, whereas implicit memory refers to unintentional or nonconscious retrieval [14], [15]. Explicit memory can be further subdivided into domains including working memory, immediate memory, learning rate, semantic organization, forgetting rate, sensitivity to interference, retrograde amnesia, and prospective memory [8]. 
Accelerated forgetting rates, reflecting encoding limitations, can be assessed using standardized verbal learning and memory tests, such as the California Verbal Learning Test (CVLT), the Rey Auditory Verbal Learning Test (AVLT), and the Logical Memory subtests of the Wechsler Memory Scale (WMS) [8]. Among these, the California Verbal Learning Test Second Edition Short Form (CVLT-II SF) is particularly sensitive to encoding deficits and long-delayed free recall performance [16]. The maximum long-delayed free recall score in the CVLT-II SF is 16 [17]. Reported mean long-delayed recall scores of approximately 11.72 for healthy controls and 8.12 for TBI patients [18] illustrate a substantial reduction in recall performance associated with encoding impairments. These clinical benchmarks provide a reference framework for evaluating computational simulations of memory performance. Rather than reproducing exact clinical scores, the present study uses these values as conceptual targets to guide interpretation of model behavior and parameter sensitivity.
Building on prior efforts to understand TBI mechanisms and recovery [19], [20], the present study investigates how key encoding-related parameters in a Nengo-based neural model influence memory performance. Specifically, we examine the number of neurons, the dimensionality of neural representations, and the scaling of input information. The number of neurons corresponds to population size, influencing coding fidelity through population averaging [22], [23]. The dimensionality represents the number of independent features used to encode each memory item; higher-dimensional representations correspond to richer population codes, allowing more distinct and robust memory traces, reducing interference and enhancing recall accuracy [24], [25]. The scale parameter modulates the amplitude of inputs to neural ensembles, analogous to gain modulation in cortical circuits. Increasing scale amplifies input strength, enhancing representational fidelity, whereas decreasing scale attenuates input, potentially degrading memory encoding [21]. Networks with recurrent feedback, similar to the ensemble structure used here, naturally support short-term memory by maintaining neuronal responses across delay periods [21]. Salinas and Sejnowski (2001) demonstrated that manipulating gain parameters affects the strength of representation and network memory capacity, providing a computational analogue of biophysical mechanisms underlying working memory [21].
Previous research has extensively examined how neuronal population size [22], [23] and representational dimensionality [24], [25] influence memory and cognitive performance. Increasing the number of neurons improves coding fidelity, while higher-dimensional representations enhance separability and encoding capacity. In this context, the scale parameter acts as an additional factor influencing memory encoding through modulation of input strength, consistent with gain modulation mechanisms observed in biological neural circuits [25]. Investigating these three parameters in the model allows systematic examination of how encoding deficits contribute to impaired recall, as often observed in TBI.
Experimental Design
To investigate the impact of encoding deficits on memory performance following TBI, a simplified spiking neural model was constructed in Nengo, inspired by the California Verbal Learning Test Second Edition Short Form (CVLT-II SF). The model comprises three layers: an input layer, a neural encoding layer, and an output layer.
Word list construction was designed to mimic the verbal presentation used in the CVLT-II SF. A total of 16 target words were selected from four semantic categories (fruits, clothing, flowers, herbs), with four words per category [26]. To ensure that no consecutive words belonged to the same category [27], a custom shuffle algorithm was applied, randomly ordering the categories while preventing repeats. This sequence of 16 words became the primary stimulus.
To facilitate decoding of temporal order information during recall, a set of order words (ONE through SIXTEEN) was also included. Additionally, a large set of background English words (2,000 randomly sampled words) was added to expand the vocabulary space and provide a richer representational context. Each word was assigned a high-dimensional semantic pointer generated from a normalized random vector in numbers of dimensions assigned. These vectors were then stored in the model vocabulary for encoding and decoding.
The encoding phase is conducted as follows. Each of the 16 target words was presented sequentially over 12–14 seconds to emulate verbal presentation in the CVLT-II SF. Words were encoded as vectorized semantic pointers in spiking neural ensembles, while their order information was represented in separate direct-mode ensembles. Circular convolution operations bound word and order representations, which were then stored in a recurrent encoding ensemble. Memory maintenance relied on recurrent connections with a low-pass synaptic filter (τ = 0.3), allowing sustained activity over a simulated 30-second long-delay interval without introducing biologically realistic decay or stochastic noise.
For the recall, after the 30-second delay interval, the network’s activity was probed using sim.data[probe] to evaluate recall. Recall was assessed by decoding the stored representations from the recurrent ensemble at the time point corresponding to the end of the delay. The absolute time for recall was calculated as the end of the encoding phase plus the delay (t_abs = n_words * word_duration + delay) and converted to the nearest simulation index. The network activity at this index was treated as the encoded representation of all words. For each word in the original 16-word list, its corresponding order vector was retrieved from the vocabulary, and the encoded network representation was unbound from this order vector using circular convolution with the inverse, isolating the semantic content of the word associated with that temporal position. The resulting unbound vector was then compared to all vectors in the vocabulary using cosine similarity, and the word with the highest similarity score was taken as the model’s recall for that position. Finally, the recalled words were compared to the original stimulus sequence, and recall accuracy was calculated as the proportion of words correctly recalled in their correct order. This procedure ensured that recall captured both the content of the encoded representation and its temporal order, directly mapping the network’s high-dimensional semantic pointers back to discrete word outputs.
Then, a series of simulation experiments were conducted to examine the effects of three key model parameters: the number of neurons in encoding ensembles, the dimensionality of the semantic vector space, and the scale factor applied to input word vectors. Each parameter was varied independently while keeping the other two fixed, allowing isolation of its effect on recall accuracy. Memory performance was measured as the proportion of words correctly recalled in their presented order. After probing the network at a given delay, the network activity was decoded into semantic pointers, unbound from the expected order representations, and compared to all stored words using cosine similarity.
Baseline experiments established a reference configuration for evaluating parameter effects. In this baseline, ensembles contained 500 neurons, semantic vectors were 512-dimensional, and the input scale was 3.33. Recall accuracy was measured at multiple delays from 0 to 30 seconds, with a timestep of 0.001 seconds to ensure high temporal resolution. No additional noise was introduced, allowing verification that the recurrent network could preserve representations over 30 seconds without significant decay. Delays beyond 30 seconds were not examined due to practical constraints, as simulations with larger parameter values and longer durations incurred substantial computational cost.
Subsequent simulations systematically varied each parameter to determine its independent influence on accuracy. Candidate configurations were selected if they satisfied three criteria: (1) mean accuracy greater than or equal to the baseline mean, (2) variability constrained to a standard error of the mean (SEM) no greater than 2.5 times the baseline SEM, and (3) relative deviation from baseline accuracy below 10% to exclude unstable or disproportionate effects.
Statistical analyses were performed using repeated-measures ANOVA to evaluate parameter effects on recall accuracy across trials. Significance was assessed at α = 0.05 using p-values, and effect sizes were quantified with partial eta squared (η²ₚ), representing the proportion of variance attributable to each parameter. These analyses enabled systematic evaluation of how neuron number, representational dimensionality, and input scaling influence encoding fidelity and memory performance.
Finally, selected candidate configurations were run to compare their recall accuracy directly with baseline performance. Although the simplified architecture constrains absolute performance, these experiments were designed to identify which parameters most strongly enhance encoding stability and to inform future extensions incorporating more biologically realistic or large-scale network dynamics.

Analysis and Discussion of Results
Across thirty trials of baseline model runs, accuracy remained largely stable across delays from 0 to 30 seconds, indicating that the recurrent ensemble with low-pass synaptic filtering was sufficient to maintain representations over the simulated long-delay interval. Statistical analysis confirmed no significant effect of delay on accuracy (p = 0.3758, η²ₚ = 0.0096) (Fig. 1).
When evaluating the effects of individual parameters on recall accuracy at a 30-second delay, changes in the number of neurons had a minimal and non-significant influence (p = 0.331987, η²ₚ = 0.116799). Accuracy fluctuated irregularly across tested neuron counts, with intermediate values producing slight, inconsistent improvements, suggesting that simply increasing neuron numbers does not reliably enhance memory performance in this model.
In contrast, representational dimensionality had a strong and highly significant effect (p = 1.182644e-15, η²ₚ = 0.889174). As shown in Fig. 4, accuracy increased approximately linearly with higher-dimensional representations, demonstrating that expanding the network’s representational capacity enhances separability and fidelity of stored word vectors, directly improving recall performance. The scale parameter also significantly influenced performance (p = 3.270404e-11, η²ₚ = 0.407147) (Fig. 3). Moderate reductions in scale relative to the baseline (3.33) tended to increase accuracy, whereas increases beyond the baseline decreased performance, indicating that the magnitude of input signals modulates the strength of encoded representations and directly impacts recall.
Together, these findings suggest that while the number of neurons contributes only marginally, both representational dimensionality and input scaling are critical determinants of memory performance in the current recurrent network. The linear effect of dimensionality, the inverse trend with scale, and the fluctuating influence of neuron count highlight how architectural choices shape encoding efficacy in this simplified model.
To further examine performance differences over time, one candidate configuration that met the predefined selection criteria (n_neurons = 500, dim = 768, scale = 3.66) was compared directly to the baseline across delays (Fig. 5). At a delay of 0 seconds, the candidate configuration produced a higher mean accuracy (16.25%, SEM = 1.381927) compared to the baseline (15.21%, SEM = 0.648504), indicating an initial performance advantage of approximately 1%. Over the 30-second delay, candidate performance converged slightly but remained higher overall, yielding a mean accuracy of 15.625% (SEM = 1.397542) compared to the baseline mean of 15.417% (SEM = 0.651957). The smaller SEM observed in the baseline condition is attributable to the larger number of trials (30 trials) relative to the candidate simulations (10 trials), which naturally reduces sampling variability. Despite this difference in variability, the candidate configuration maintained a consistently higher mean accuracy across the retention interval.
These results indicate that limitations in the encoding process, rather than decay over the retention interval, are the primary drivers of reduced recall accuracy. The strong effects of vector dimensionality and input scaling emphasize that the richness of neural representations and the strength of input signals critically determine how effectively words are encoded. Low-dimensional representations or suboptimal signal scaling can lead to overlapping or weakly activated patterns, resulting in poorer recall. Conversely, merely increasing neuron count without improving dimensionality or input strength does not reliably enhance encoding, underscoring that deficits in recall accuracy reflect representational quality more than network size.
Notably, these findings align with prior research in cognitive neuroscience regarding the relationships between memory encoding, neuronal population size, representational dimensionality, and signal scaling [21]–[25]. The observation that vector dimensionality exerts the greatest impact on recall supports the view that memory performance is more strongly determined by representational richness and encoding capacity than by retention duration alone.
Conclusion
The present study extends the original research proposal, which aimed to simulate neural injury and recovery mechanisms in cortical microcircuits following traumatic brain injury (TBI). Due to practical and computational constraints, the focus shifted from modeling multiple forms of neural damage (e.g., connection severing, activation noise, weight perturbation, weight scaling) to systematically examining how core encoding-related parameters, number of neurons, vector dimensionality, and input scaling, affect memory performance within a controlled recurrent spiking network. This adjustment allowed isolation of encoding mechanisms before introducing explicit injury manipulations.
Within the current deterministic architecture, recall accuracy remained stable across a 30-second delay, indicating that recurrent connectivity with low-pass synaptic filtering was sufficient to maintain representations over time. Delay itself did not significantly affect performance. Instead, encoding capacity and representational quality emerged as the primary determinants of recall accuracy. Representational dimensionality exerted the largest and most consistent effect, showing a near-linear improvement in performance as dimensionality increased. Input scaling also significantly influenced accuracy, with moderate reductions relative to baseline improving performance, while excessive amplification reduced it. In contrast, increasing neuron count produced fluctuating and non-systematic effects, suggesting that network size alone does not reliably enhance encoding fidelity. Even when a candidate configuration (n_neurons = 500, dim = 768, scale = 3.66) was tested across delays, it produced only modest improvements over baseline, reinforcing the conclusion that architectural constraints, rather than parameter tuning alone, limit overall performance.
These findings suggest that memory impairments may be more strongly associated with reduced representational richness or weakened encoding strength than with passive temporal decay or simple reductions in neuron count. In this model, deficits arise primarily from limitations in how information is encoded rather than how long it is retained.
However, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the model does not incorporate stochastic neural noise, synaptic degradation, or biologically realistic decay processes. Consequently, the absence of pronounced delay-dependent declines in recall cannot be interpreted as evidence that retention deficits are unimportant in TBI; rather, it reflects the deterministic nature of the current implementation and the reliance on low-pass synaptic filtering (τ = 0.3) to maintain activity over the simulated 30-second delay. Second, only a subset of parameter configurations was explored due to computational constraints, and a full hyperparameter optimization approach (e.g., Optuna) was not conducted.
Importantly, memory encoding in the model is abstracted using high-dimensional semantic vectors, with words represented as vectorized semantic pointers rather than as auditory phonological streams or visual orthographic inputs. As such, the results do not reflect modality-specific encoding and should not be interpreted as modeling auditory or visual presentation directly. In the context of the CVLT-II SF, which verbally presents 16 words over 12–14 seconds, the model captures modality-independent, conceptual encoding of word content and order. Real-world TBI-related deficits may vary depending on the sensory modality of the stimulus, as auditory and visual inputs rely on partially distinct cortical pathways and processing dynamics. The current architecture does not differentiate these pathways and therefore represents generalized encoding capacity rather than modality-specific mechanisms.
Additionally, the simplified microcircuit architecture does not capture large-scale interactions among brain regions, such as left frontal areas supporting encoding and right superior frontal areas contributing to retrieval [28]. While the model provides insight into how architectural parameters, such as neural population size, representational dimensionality, and gain-like input scaling, affect recall accuracy, it does not fully represent the distributed neural circuits underlying explicit memory in humans. Furthermore, candidate parameter exploration was conducted to assess relative effects on recall accuracy, but the network architecture itself limits the ability to achieve human-level performance (~73% recall).
In summary, this study establishes a mechanistic foundation for understanding how encoding-related architectural parameters influence recall in a simplified recurrent network. Although it does not yet implement stochastic noise, structural injury, or modality-specific pathways, it provides a framework for future work incorporating biologically realistic decay, multimodal inputs, and network-level interactions, enabling closer alignment with clinical TBI data and more realistic modeling of encoding and retrieval deficits.
